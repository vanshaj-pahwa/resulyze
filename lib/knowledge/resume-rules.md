# Resume Writing Rules & Best Practices

A comprehensive knowledge base for AI-assisted resume writing, reviewing, and optimization. Every rule here is grounded in how hiring actually works — what recruiters scan for in 30 seconds, what ATS systems parse, and what hiring managers care about.

---

## Core Principle

A resume is a 30-second elevator pitch on paper. It must be succinct, easy to skim, and packed with relevant, technical substance. Its only job is to get you an interview. Nothing more.

A resume can only do so much without genuine, relevant experience behind it.

---

## Formatting Rules

### Layout & Structure

- Single-column layout only. No multi-column, no sidebars, no two-column designs.
- No icons, images, graphics, logos, or photos.
- No indentation on sections or bullet points. Bullet characters already create visual indentation.
- Use bullet points, never paragraphs.
- Do not justify text — it creates inconsistent word spacing. Use left-aligned text.
- One page maximum unless 10+ years of experience. Rule of thumb: 1 page per decade.

### Typography

- Use a modern, readable font: Calibri, Bitstream Charter, Arial, Lato, Helvetica.
- Minimum font size: 10.5pt. Use black (not thin/light weight) fonts.
- Do not use grey-colored fonts. Ensure sufficient contrast with white background and printability in greyscale.
- Do not italicize body text — it decreases readability.
- Do not bold keywords within bullet points — it's distracting.
- Italicization, bolding, and ALL-CAPS should be used sparingly and independently of each other. Excessive use defeats their purpose (emphasis).

### Spacing & Margins

- Minimum margins: 0.4 inches on all sides.
- Minimum line spacing: 1.07.
- Ensure sufficient and consistent white space between sections, subsections, and entries.
- Clear section separation — the reader must instantly distinguish one section from another.
- Never cram content. White space is not wasted space.

### Dates

- Currently working: use "Present" (not "Current," "Now," or "Ongoing").
- Use months + years or years only. Do not mix in seasons or semesters.
- Do not abbreviate years ("'23"). Write the full year: "2023".
- Do not use digit-format months ("9/2013"). Write "Sept 2013".
- Month abbreviations: Jan, Feb, Mar, Apr, May, June, July, Aug, Sept, Oct, Nov, Dec.
- Use en dashes for ranges with spaces: "Mar 2022 – Present" (not hyphens or "to").
- Right-align dates to the right margin.
- Bullet point text should not extend further right than the dates.

### Skills Formatting

- Separate skills using commas, not pipes, dashes, or slashes.
- Capitalize skill names correctly: SolidWorks, CATIA V5, LabVIEW, JavaScript, TypeScript, PostgreSQL.
- Separate similar but distinct skills: "C, C++" not "C/C++".
- 3 lines or fewer in a single-column format.
- Order skills from most relevant/important to least.
- Group into categories: Languages, Frameworks, Tools, Databases, Cloud, etc.

## Section Order

### Experienced professionals (post-graduation, full-time job):
1. Work Experience > Skills > Education, or
2. Skills > Work Experience > Education

### Students / new grads with limited work experience:
1. Education > Work Experience > Skills, or
2. Education > Work Experience > Projects > Skills

### No technical work experience:
1. Education > Projects > Work Experience > Skills

### No work experience at all:
1. Education > Projects > Volunteer Experience / Extracurriculars > Skills

### Rules:
- A summary/profile is optional. It is most impactful for senior/staff engineers, career changers, or candidates addressing an employment gap. For others, it can still be included — if present, it should be a sharp 2–3 line professional summary that hooks a recruiter in under 10 seconds. Never remove a profile section without the user's explicit permission — instead, advise on whether to keep or remove it and explain the trade-off.
- Do NOT include a references section.

## Contact Information

### Include:
- Full name
- One email address (Gmail or Outlook — not AOL, Hotmail, Yahoo)
- GitHub profile URL (only if it has real, maintained projects with READMEs)
- Portfolio/personal website URL (only if up-to-date)

### Formatting:
- Write URLs in plain text: "github.com/username" — not masked links.
- Do not prefix with "Email:", "GitHub:", "Portfolio:".
- Do not include "https://www." in URLs.
- Do not underline, italicize, or color URLs.

### Do NOT include:
- Physical address or ZIP code.
- City/state location (unless the job is specifically in that city — non-local location causes bias).
- LinkedIn profile (recruiters can search by name; it rarely gets clicked).
- Phone number (interviews start with email; non-local area codes cause bias).
  - If included: no prefix ("Phone:", "Cell:"), no country code for US/Canada.

### Special cases:
- Security clearance: list near your name (e.g., "Top Secret / SCI eligible with CI Polygraph").
- Work authorization: if name or background doesn't make it obvious, add "US Citizen" or "Canadian Citizen" near your name to prevent sponsorship assumptions.

## Work Experience

### Section naming:
- Use "Work Experience" or "Experience."
- NOT: "Professional Experience," "Selected Experience," "Employment History," "Employment," "Job History."

### Rules:
- Only include PAID work experience (research experience is acceptable).
- Clearly label internships as internships and contract positions as contracts.
- Order positions by relevance to target job OR by impressiveness — best stuff first.
- Tailor for each application to address the specific job description.
- Differentiate yourself. Your resume is not your job description. Focus on accomplishments, metrics, and technical victories — not duties.
- Add context. Buzzwords alone are insufficient. Explain what you did and how you solved problems.
- Focus on engineering skills, not just tools. Anyone can learn SolidWorks clicks — fewer people can identify capability gaps, create preliminary designs, and execute the full design process.

## Bullet Points

### Objectives:
1. Highlight the technical work you did.
2. Highlight technical challenges you faced and overcame.
3. Highlight the impact of your work.

### Structure — Follow STAR, XYZ, or CAR:

**STAR** — Situation, Task, Action, Results
- What was the context? What needed to be done? What did you do? What happened?

**XYZ** — Accomplished [X] as measured by [Y], by doing [Z]
- "Reduced API response time by 40% by implementing Redis caching and query optimization"

**CAR** — Challenge, Action, Result
- What problem existed? What did you do? What was the outcome?

If you can quantify results, move the metrics toward the start of the bullet.

### Format rules:
- 1-2 lines per bullet. Aim for 1 sentence.
- Order bullets from most relevant/impressive to least within each role.
- No personal pronouns: I, we, us, my, our, their.
- No periods at the end of bullet points.
- Do not spill onto a new line with only 1-4 words. Extreme waste of space.
- Do not allow words to wrap and get hyphenated.
- Avoid sub-bullet points — they clutter and add verbosity.
- Avoid apostrophes, ampersands (&), and slashes (/).
- Use digits, not spelled-out numbers: "8" not "eight."

### Action verbs:
- Each bullet MUST begin with a strong, past-tense action verb.

**Strong verbs:** Analyzed, Architected, Automated, Built, Created, Decreased, Designed, Developed, Implemented, Improved, Optimized, Published, Reduced, Refactored, Integrated, Migrated, Deployed, Configured, Debugged, Delivered, Tested, Benchmarked, Scaled

**Weak verbs (avoid):** Aided, Assisted, Coded, Collaborated, Communicated, Executed, Helped, Participated, Programmed, Ran, Used, Utilized, Worked on, Exposed to, Gained experience

**Overblown verbs (avoid):** Amplified, Conceptualized, Crafted, Elevated, Engineered, Enhanced, Ensured, Fostered, Headed, Honed, Innovated, Mastered, Orchestrated, Perfected, Pioneered, Revolutionized, Spearheaded, Transformed

**Frequently misused:** Leverage, Enhance (vs. Improve), Utilize (use "Use" instead — almost always clearer)

### Avoid superfluous language:
- No adjectives/adverbs that add nothing: "excellent," "innovative," "expert," "successfully," "creatively," "diligently," "meticulously," "strategically."
- If the bullet needs an adverb to sound impressive, the achievement itself isn't impressive enough. Fix the achievement, not the decoration.

### Content generation — ask yourself:
1. **What did I do?** Did I solve a problem? Independent or team? How big was the team?
2. **How did I do it?** What tools, frameworks, methodologies? Why that approach? Any testing/validation?
3. **What were the results?** On time? Under budget? Did it work? What metrics improved? What did I learn?

### Integration matters:
- Don't list a "parts list" of technologies. Explain how components worked together.
- Bad: "Used React, Redux, and Node.js"
- Good: "Built a real-time dashboard using React with Redux state management, backed by a Node.js WebSocket server that processed 10K+ events/second"

## Education

- Do NOT include coursework unless courses are extremely specialized or impressive.
- Do NOT include high school.
- Do NOT include schools from which you didn't receive a degree.
- Only include graduation date, not start date:
  - Current student: "Expected May 2025"
  - Graduated: "May 2021" or "2021"
- Reverse chronological order (Master's above Bachelor's).
- GPA: include if above 3.75. Two decimal places max. Remove once you start first full-time job (unless very impressive).
- Degree naming: "Bachelor of Science" (not "Bachelor's of Science").
- Don't include school location if it's in the school name (MIT, UCLA) or commonly known.
- Don't include awards/scholarships unless extremely impressive (Rhodes Scholar, Fulbright).
- Include D1/competitive sports.

## Skills Section

### Naming:
- Use "Skills." Not "Technical Skills," "Relevant Skills," "Core Competencies."

### Include:
- Languages you could interview in.
- Technologies (frameworks, tools, programs) you've actually used.
- Skills that appear in your bullet points (and vice versa — consistency matters).

### Do NOT include:
- Soft skills (teamwork, leadership, communication). Demonstrate these through bullet points.
- Skills taken for granted: typing, Microsoft Word, IDEs, basic computer literacy.
- Code hosting platforms as skills. Use "Git" not "GitHub" or "GitLab."
- Operating systems.
- IDEs or text editors (VS Code, vim, etc.).
- Do not use descriptors: no "Expert in," "Professional in," "Proficient in." List the skill only.

### Ordering:
- Most important/relevant skills first.
- If a skill isn't important enough to be near the top, consider removing it entirely.

---

## Projects Section

### Naming:
- Use "Projects." Not "Academic Projects," "Personal Projects," "Technical Projects," etc.

### Rules:
- For personal/extracurricular/hobby projects only. Work projects go under Experience.
- Don't use the word "project" in project titles — it's redundant.
- Capitalize titles correctly.
- Include links to portfolio pages and/or GitHub repos for personal projects.
- No need to label as "Personal Project" or "Academic Project."
- Use bullet points, not paragraphs.
- Order by relevance to target job and general impressiveness.
- No roles/positions/locations/dates needed for personal projects.

### What makes projects stand out:
- Real projects that solve real problems, not tutorial follow-alongs.
- Projects with actual users (even if just yourself, as long as actively maintained).
- Student competition teams (SAE, AIAA, rocketry, FRC mentoring).

## ATS (Applicant Tracking Systems)

### What ATS actually does:
- Stores and organizes applications in a database.
- Allows recruiters to search/filter by keywords.
- Does NOT auto-reject based on formatting (this is a myth).
- Does NOT score resumes (another myth — recruiters do the screening).

### ATS-friendly practices:
- Standard section headings that any system can parse.
- Simple single-column layout.
- No tables, columns, text boxes, or graphics embedded in the document.
- Consistent date formatting.
- Keywords from the job description naturally integrated into bullets and skills.
- PDF format (most ATS parse PDF well; some prefer .docx — when in doubt, submit PDF).

### ATS myths to ignore:
- "ATS rejects resumes with bad formatting" — false. Humans reject resumes.
- "You need to stuff keywords" — false. Keyword stuffing makes resumes unreadable to humans.
- "ATS can't read PDFs" — false. Modern ATS handles PDF fine.
- "You need a specific ATS score" — there is no universal ATS score. Different systems work differently.

## Page Length

### Why one page:
- Most resumes don't need a second page. Once cleaned up, many people struggle to fill one page with relevant content.
- A multi-page resume with poor formatting and irrelevant content gives a bad impression.
- Hiring teams spend less effort on longer resumes.
- If you're having trouble fitting on one page, remove less relevant content (even if technical). Save it for your portfolio.

### When two pages are acceptable:
- Senior/staff roles with 10+ years of relevant experience.
- The content on page two must be genuinely strong and relevant.
- Make earlier roles more concise. Recent roles get more space.

---

## Senior Engineers (10+ YoE)

- Consider a brief summary (under 2 sentences).
- Maintain separate resumes for management vs. IC positions.
- Stay under 2 pages.
- Mention soft achievements alongside business results.
- Highlight not just impact but influence (cross-team, org-wide).
- Make earlier roles concise — focus detail on recent/relevant roles.
- Move education to the bottom.

---

## Career Changers

- Include a 2-sentence summary explaining the career change and motivation.
- Link to working projects and source code.
- Be concise about past work experience.
- Fit on 1 page.

## Bias Prevention

Do NOT include on your resume:
- Age or date of birth
- Gender
- Marital status or children
- Nationality or ethnicity (unless work authorization is relevant)
- Religion
- Political views
- Photo

## Grammar & Proofreading

- Spell out obscure abbreviations the first time: "scanning tunneling microscope (STM)."
- Only capitalize proper nouns.
- Run grammar check, not just spell check.
- Typos and poor grammar signal lack of attention to detail and can cause immediate rejection.
- Have someone else proofread — fresh eyes catch what you miss.

---

## Sensitive / Classified Content

When describing work on classified or proprietary projects:
- Describe the technologies and engineering approaches without revealing specifics.
- Bad: "Developed C++ software for testing LANTIRN display pod on the F-14D Tomcat"
- Good: "Developed C++ testing software for evaluating aircraft subsystems on MIL-STD-based test benches"
- Focus on the engineering skills demonstrated, not the classified details.
- If asked for more detail in an interview, you can explain the boundary.

## LaTeX-Specific Rules

When writing or editing LaTeX resumes, the AI should:

1. **Maintain single-column layout.** Never introduce multicol, minipage side-by-side layouts, or paracol.
2. **Keep it on one page.** If content overflows, trim low-impact bullets before adjusting spacing. Never reduce font below 10.5pt.
3. **Use standard section commands.** `\section{Experience}`, `\section{Education}`, `\section{Skills}`, `\section{Projects}`. No creative section names.
4. **Bullet points via itemize.** Use `\begin{itemize}` with `\item`, not manual dashes or hyphens.
5. **Dates right-aligned.** Use `\hfill` or tabular alignment to push dates to the right margin.
6. **No graphics packages.** No tikz decorations, no includegraphics, no fontawesome icons in the resume body.
7. **Consistent date formatting.** En dashes with spaces: `Mar 2022 -- Present` in LaTeX (renders as en dash).
8. **Clean compilation.** Every edit must produce valid LaTeX. Never leave unmatched braces, missing `\end{}` tags, or undefined commands.
9. **Readable source.** Use comments to separate sections. Consistent indentation. No unnecessary blank lines.
10. **Hyperref for links.** Use `\href{url}{text}` for email and URLs. Do not display raw URLs with protocol.
11. **Margins via geometry.** Use `\usepackage{geometry}` with minimum 0.4in margins.
12. **No page numbers.** Use `\pagenumbering{gobble}` or `\pagestyle{empty}`.
13. **Font size.** Body text at 10.5pt-11pt minimum. Name can be larger. Section headings slightly larger than body.
14. **Spacing control.** Use `\vspace{}` and `\setlength{\itemsep}{}` for spacing. Prefer content trimming over spacing reduction.
